Research: Danish South / German Baltic Coast

Wild card destination option for the June 2026 family road trip from Delft.

This is the furthest option on the list. The appeal is a different cultural world – Danish hygge, Viking history, stunning Baltic coastline, and a quieter pace of life. The question is whether it delivers enough to justify the extra driving.

1. Best Base Towns

German Baltic Coast

Flensburg

  • Population ~90,000. Germany’s northernmost city, sitting on a fjord just 7 km from the Danish border.

  • Was Danish for 400 years until 1864. Still has a real Danish feel – Danish-language signs, Danish bakeries, a Danish minority community.

  • Compact, walkable old town with a beautiful harbor lined with converted warehouses (now restaurants and shops). The Museumswerft (open-air shipyard museum) lets kids watch craftsmen building traditional wooden boats. There is a children’s boatyard.

  • Has its own beach at Wassersleben – white sand, shallow water, minimal tide, seaside playground.

  • Rum history from the Danish West Indies trade era. A Rum Museum documents this.

  • As a family base: Solid option. Real town with daily life, not a resort. Good walkability. Close to the Danish border so you can day-trip into Denmark easily. The downside is that it is a mid-sized city, not a village – less “charming cottage” feel, more “historic port town.”

  • Drive from Delft: ~5.5-6 hours direct.

Lubeck

  • UNESCO World Heritage old town. Classic Hanseatic city with brick Gothic architecture, the iconic Holstentor gate, narrow medieval lanes.

  • Extremely walkable old town on an island surrounded by water. Beautiful and historically rich.

  • Known for marzipan (Niederegger – kids will love this).

  • Not directly on the coast – Travemunde (its beach suburb) is 20 minutes away.

  • As a family base: Gorgeous town to explore but feels more like a city visit than a beach base. Better as a day trip from a coastal base than as the base itself.

  • Drive from Delft: ~5 hours direct.

Travemunde

  • Lubeck’s seaside district, a resort since 1802. Idyllic old town with half-timbered houses and brick gabled buildings.

  • Long sandy beach with a promenade. Germany’s largest Baltic ferry port (big ships coming and going, which kids tend to find exciting).

  • Walkable waterfront path. Pleasant but has a “German beach resort” feel – think Strandkorb wicker beach chairs, ice cream shops, tourist shops.

  • As a family base: Good beach access, charming enough center, but lacks the cultural distinctiveness that makes this destination a “wild card.” It is fundamentally a well-run German beach town.

  • Drive from Delft: ~5 hours direct.

Timmendorfer Strand

  • Classic German Baltic beach resort, 15 km north of Lubeck. 7 km of fine sandy beach with shallow water. Ten lifeguard stations.

  • Compact and walkable – beach to town center on foot. Upscale promenade with good restaurants and boutiques.

  • Sea Life aquarium (sharks, rays, tropical fish) – reliable kid attraction.

  • Neighboring Scharbeutz has a similar beach setup.

  • As a family base: Excellent beach infrastructure, very family-oriented. But this is a resort town, not a discovery destination. You would not come here for cultural depth. Good for beach days, less interesting for the wandering-and-exploring the family enjoys.

  • Drive from Delft: ~5 hours direct.

Kiel Area

  • State capital of Schleswig-Holstein. Sits on the Kiel Fjord, an inlet of the Baltic.

  • Maritime museum, aquarium, a WWII submarine museum (U-995). Kiellinie promenade along the fjord.

  • Falckensteiner Strand – longest city beach, with views of ocean liners passing the lighthouse.

  • As a family base: More of a city than a vacation base. Better as a stop than a home for the week. Not charming enough for what you are looking for.

  • Drive from Delft: ~5.5 hours direct.

Danish Towns

Ribe (top recommendation for this region)

  • The oldest town in Denmark (and Scandinavia). Founded ~700 AD. Medieval center with crooked cobblestone streets, timber-framed houses, a towering cathedral.

  • Extremely compact and walkable. No car needed once you arrive. The whole town feels like stepping back in time – not in a museum way, in a “people still live here” way.

  • Ribe VikingeCenter (3 km south of town): One of the largest Viking museums in Scandinavia. Staff in period clothing bake bread over fires, demonstrate archery, do falconry shows, and run “warrior training” for kids with swords and shields. Two play areas. Families consistently rate it as excellent – immersive, educational, not gimmicky. This is the kind of thing your kids would talk about for years.

  • Ribe Cathedral has panoramic views from its tower.

  • Close to the Wadden Sea (UNESCO World Heritage). Close to Romo island (30 min drive).

  • 45 minutes from Legoland Billund if you want a day trip.

  • As a family base: This is the standout. Genuinely charming, walkable, historically rich, great for kids, and well-positioned for day trips. The only downside is that it is inland – you need to drive to the coast (Romo is 30 min, the Wadden Sea is closer). Not a “walk out the door to the beach” situation.

  • Drive from Delft: ~6-6.5 hours (the longest option).

Romo Island (Romo)

  • Connected to the mainland by a causeway (no ferry needed). One of Europe’s widest beaches – up to 4 km wide at low tide, 8.5 km long. You can drive your car onto the sand.

  • Two main beaches: Lakolk (swimming and relaxation) and Sonderstrand (activities, land sailing, kite flying).

  • Tonnissgard Nature Centre: mudflat walks, shrimp fishing, amber cutting workshops, kite making. Guided boat tours of the tidal ecosystem.

  • Flat terrain, great for cycling. Small island, no major elevations.

  • As a family base: The beach is genuinely extraordinary – unlike anything in the Netherlands or Normandy. But the island itself is quiet. Very quiet. Limited restaurants, limited walkable village life. Great for beach days and nature, but you may feel isolated for a full week. Best paired with Ribe as a nearby town for evenings or day trips.

  • Drive from Delft: ~6-6.5 hours.

Aero Island

  • Small island in the South Funen Archipelago. Accessible only by ferry (~1h15 from Svendborg). Rick Steves calls it one of his “favorite island getaways in Europe.”

  • Aeroskobing is the main town – often called the “fairytale town.” Colorful houses, cobbled streets. Entirely walkable. No car needed.

  • Famous colorful beach huts at Vesterstrand just outside town – look like something from a children’s book.

  • 22 miles x 6 miles. Best explored by bike. Free public bus.

  • As a family base: Incredibly charming but logistically difficult for a family of 5 with a car. You need to take a ferry from the mainland, and the island has limited services. Better as a day trip or overnight than a week-long base. The ferry schedule constrains your flexibility. With three young kids, that adds friction you probably do not want.

  • Drive from Delft: ~6+ hours to the ferry, plus the 1h15 crossing. Not realistic as a base.

Sonderborg (on Als Island)

  • Sits on the Als fjord. Connected to the mainland by bridge (easy access). Sonderborg Castle dates to the 13th century – free admission for under 18, exhibitions on regional history and folk culture.

  • Walkable, flat, and small. Harbor front is lively – marina views, cafes. A proper town with daily life.

  • Universe adventure park nearby: science, technology, rappelling, segway, 5D cinema, laser track. More engaging for the 7-9 age range.

  • Grasten Castle (royal summer residence) is nearby.

  • As a family base: Decent option – walkable, kid-friendly castle, good harbor vibe. But it does not have the same “wow” factor as Ribe, and the beach access is more fjord than open coast. It is 1.5 hours from Legoland, which is a stretch. Feels like a solid B+ rather than an A.

  • Drive from Delft: ~6 hours.

Tonder

  • Small border town (~8,000 people), 5 km from the German border. Half-timbered old town, cobblestone lanes.

  • Walkable and bike-friendly. Textile museum, old water tower with views.

  • Paired with Romo (the island is nearby), this could work as a land-based base with beach access.

  • As a family base: Charming but very small. Limited restaurant scene. Works if you want maximum quiet and authenticity, but a full week here might feel long. Better as part of a multi-base trip.

  • Drive from Delft: ~5.5-6 hours.

2. Beach Quality

Baltic Sea vs. North Sea – Key Differences

Water Temperature (Mid-June):

  • Baltic Sea: 15-18C (59-64F), with sheltered bays occasionally reaching 20-23C in warm spells

  • North Sea (Dutch coast): 14-16C (57-61F)

  • Verdict: Baltic is slightly warmer. Neither is warm. Kids will splash regardless.

Waves and Safety:

  • Baltic Sea: Non-tidal. Calm, gentle. Beaches slope gradually into the water. Ideal for small children – no sudden drops, no strong currents, no big waves.

  • North Sea: Tidal, choppier, stronger currents. More dramatic but less child-friendly for free play.

  • Verdict: Clear advantage to the Baltic for young kids.

Sand Quality:

  • German Baltic (Timmendorfer Strand, Travemunde): Fine white sand. Well-maintained. Classic European beach resort quality.

  • Romo (Danish North Sea side): Extraordinary – hard-packed sand so wide you can drive on it. Unlike any beach in the Netherlands. Dramatic and vast.

  • Danish Baltic beaches: Good quality but less dramatic.

Water Quality:

  • Baltic: Brackish (lower salt content). Gentler on eyes and skin. Blue Flag certified at many beaches.

  • North Sea: Full salt water.

The Honest Take: The Baltic beaches are better for small children than the North Sea. Calmer, warmer (slightly), and more predictable. Romo’s North Sea beach is in a category of its own – genuinely spectacular in scale – but it is a North Sea beach with tides and wind. The German Baltic resort beaches (Timmendorfer Strand, Scharbeutz) are the most “complete” beach experiences with lifeguards, facilities, and cafes. Danish beaches tend to be wilder and less developed, which is either a plus or minus depending on your taste.

3. Food Scene

Danish Food

Danish food culture is distinctive and appealing:

  • Smorrebrod (open-faced sandwiches): Dense dark rye bread with beautiful toppings – herring, shrimp, roast beef, egg. Ubiquitous at lunch. Kids may or may not go for it, but it is genuinely excellent.

  • Seafood: Herring (many preparations), fiskefrikadeller (fish cakes), plaice, cod, shrimp. Denmark is surrounded by sea and it shows.

  • Bakeries: Danish bakeries (bageri) are outstanding. Pastries, rugbrod (rye bread), wienerbrood. This alone is a reason to go to Denmark.

  • Hotdogs: The Danish polse (hot dog) stand is a cultural institution. Kids will love this.

  • Ice cream: Danes take ice cream seriously. You will find good options in any town.

South Jutland Specifics:

  • Regional specialty: Cabbage sausage and local cheeses.

  • Ribe has a handful of good restaurants for a small town, plus cafes around the cathedral square.

  • Romo and Tonder are quieter – you will find restaurants but not a buzzing food scene.

German Baltic Food

  • Fischbrötchen (fish sandwiches) – herring, shrimp, or smoked fish on a roll. Everywhere on the coast. Simple and excellent.

  • Marzipan in Lubeck (Niederegger is the famous producer – the cafe is worth a visit).

  • Lubeck and Flensburg have the deepest restaurant scenes in this region.

  • Timmendorfer Strand has upscale dining along the promenade.

  • Flensburg’s harbor has good options in converted warehouses.

Honest Assessment

This is not Normandy or Provence for food. The culinary depth is real but modest. You will eat well – fresh seafood, excellent bread, good bakeries, solid casual restaurants. But you will not have the “every meal is an event” experience that France offers. The Danish bakery culture and smorrebrod tradition are genuinely special, though. Flensburg and Lubeck are the best bets for restaurant variety. On the islands (Romo, Aero) and in the smallest towns (Tonder), options thin out quickly.

Bottom line: Good food, not great food. Enough to keep you satisfied for a week, especially if you are cooking some meals in your Airbnb with excellent local ingredients (fresh fish, bread, cheese). Not a food desert, but not a food destination either.

The Danish and German towns in this region are generally flat and compact. This is one of their strengths. Ribe and Flensburg in particular reward wandering on foot – narrow lanes, harbor views, small squares. Romo is the exception: you need a car for everything.

5. Kid-Friendly Activities

The Highlights (for ages 5, 7, 9)

Viking History

  • Ribe VikingeCenter: The crown jewel. Living Viking village with warrior training (sword and shield for kids), archery, falconry shows, bread baking over open fires. Staff in full period costume. Two dedicated play areas. Plan at least half a day, possibly a full day. This is world-class for kids.

  • Viking ship replicas and artifacts at various museums in the region.

Castles

  • Sonderborg Castle: 13th century, free for under-18s. Exhibitions on folk culture and regional history.

  • Koldinghus: Impressive royal castle ruin, further north.

  • Grasten Castle: Royal summer residence (exterior only, but the gardens are open).

  • Lubeck’s Holstentor: Iconic medieval gate. Kids can see and climb.

Beaches and Water

  • Romo’s vast beach – driving on the sand, kite flying, land sailing, sandcastle building at an epic scale.

  • Lakolk beach on Romo – swimming, wading, calm areas.

  • Timmendorfer Strand – lifeguarded, shallow, safe.

  • Flensburg’s Wassersleben beach – shallow water, playground.

  • Wadden Sea mudflat walks (UNESCO) – shrimp fishing, finding crabs, guided nature tours. Genuinely exciting for kids.

Animals and Nature

  • Tonnissgard Nature Centre (Romo) – mudflat hikes for kids, shrimp fishing, amber cutting, kite making workshops.

  • Wadden Sea bird watching. In the right season, the “Black Sun” (Sort Sol) phenomenon of millions of starlings.

  • Seal watching tours from various ports.

  • Flensburg’s children’s boatyard.

Legoland Billund

  • Located ~45 minutes from Ribe. Doable as a day trip if you base in Ribe.

  • The original Legoland. Miniland (tiny Lego cities), themed rides, roller coasters scaled for kids.

  • A full day commitment. Would your kids love it? Almost certainly. Is it necessary? No – there is plenty without it. But it is there if you want it.

Boats and Ferries

  • Ferries between islands and ports. Kids generally love boat rides.

  • Flensburg harbor – watching ships, the open-air shipyard museum.

  • Kiel Canal – watching massive ships transit the canal.

Miscellaneous

  • Lubeck’s marzipan museum/cafe (Niederegger) – watching marzipan being made.

  • Universe adventure park near Sonderborg – science/tech themed, rappelling, segway, 5D cinema.

  • Sea Life aquarium at Timmendorfer Strand.

Honest Assessment for Ages 5-7-9

This region is genuinely strong for this age range. The Viking Centre alone could justify the trip for kids who are into history, adventure, or just running around in a huge outdoor space. The beaches provide natural engagement (especially Romo’s epic scale). The castles are real and atmospheric. The Wadden Sea nature activities are hands-on and immersive. There are no “manufactured” kid attractions here (aside from Legoland) – it is all real places, real history, real nature. For kids who are “good explorers,” this is ideal territory.

6. The Drive from Delft – The Critical Question

Direct Distances

Destination Distance Direct Drive Time
Timmendorfer Strand ~620 km ~5h 00m
Lubeck ~600 km ~5h 00m
Travemunde ~610 km ~5h 00m
Flensburg ~650 km ~5h 40m
Sonderborg ~680 km ~6h 00m
Ribe ~700 km ~6h 15m
Romo ~720 km ~6h 30m

Can This Be Done With Three Young Kids?

Not in one shot. A 5.5-6.5 hour drive with three kids aged 5-9 is asking for misery. You need to split this with an overnight stop. The good news: the route passes through excellent overnight cities.

Recommended Route: Delft to Ribe (with overnight)

Day 1: Delft to Bremen (~4 hours)

  • Route: A13 north, then east through the Netherlands (Utrecht corridor), cross into Germany, A1/A28 toward Bremen.

  • ~400 km. Very manageable with one rest stop.

  • Bremen overnight: Compact, walkable old town. The Schnoor Quarter has fairy-tale cobblestone lanes with half-timbered houses. Kids will want to see the Bremen Town Musicians statue (from the Brothers Grimm story). Burgerpark has playgrounds, a petting zoo, mini-golf. Universum Bremen is a clam-shaped science museum with 300+ interactive exhibits.

  • Stay near the Altstadt/old town area. This is a genuinely fun overnight, not just a highway stopover.

Day 2: Bremen to Ribe (~2.5 hours)

  • ~250 km. Easy morning drive. Cross into Denmark.

  • Arrive by lunchtime. Settle into your accommodation with the whole afternoon free.

Alternative overnight: Hamburg (~3.5 hours from Delft)

  • Slightly shorter Day 1, slightly longer Day 2 (~2.5-3 hours to Ribe).

  • Hamburg has Miniatur Wunderland (world’s largest model railway – kids of all ages love it), the Alster lake (paddle boats), Hagenbeck Zoo.

  • Stay in the HafenCity or Binnenalster canal area. Avoid areas near Hauptbahnhof and St. Pauli with kids.

  • Hamburg is a bigger city and harder to “do” in one evening than Bremen. Bremen is the better overnight for this trip.

How Many Days Does Driving Eat?

  • Day 1: Drive to Bremen (4h). Arrive by early afternoon. Explore Bremen.

  • Day 2: Drive to Ribe (2.5h). Arrive by lunch. Afternoon free.

  • Day 6 or 7: Reverse the trip. Could do it in one long day (~6h) if kids are road-trip-hardened by then, or split again with another overnight.

Total driving days: 2 on the way there, 1-2 on the way back = 3-4 days with some driving.

For a 7-day trip: That leaves 3-4 full days at your base. For an 8-day trip: 4-5 full days.

This is the fundamental tension of this destination. You lose more days to driving than with Normandy (one long day each way) or Zeeland (no driving days at all). The question is whether the destination justifies it.

Is the Driving Too Much?

Honest answer: it is on the edge. If you treat Bremen as a genuine stop (not just a sleep), the outbound journey becomes a two-day mini-adventure rather than pure transit. But you are spending more of your vacation in the car than with the closer options.

If your trip is exactly 7 days: This is tight. You get 3-4 days at base, which is not really enough to “settle into a rhythm.”

If your trip is 8-9 days: Much more comfortable. 4-5 days at base starts to feel like a real stay.

7. Accommodation

Danish Summer Houses

Denmark has a deeply established summer house (sommerhus) rental culture. This is a genuine advantage over other destinations:

  • Thousands of purpose-built vacation houses, especially along the coasts and on islands.

  • Typically detached houses with gardens, often with saunas, sometimes with pools.

  • Well-equipped kitchens. Designed for families.

  • Booking platforms: Airbnb works, but also check Feriepartner, VillaVilla, Novasol, and DanCenter – these are the established Danish holiday house agencies with huge inventories.

  • Landfolk is a newer, curated platform for higher-end Danish holiday homes.

Price Range (Mid-June, 3-bedroom, 1 week)

  • Romo / South Jutland: EUR 800-1,500/week for a comfortable 3-bedroom house. Peak season (July) is higher; mid-June is the sweet spot just before prices jump.

  • Ribe area: Fewer dedicated summer houses than the coast. More Airbnb apartments and B&Bs. EUR 700-1,200/week.

  • German Baltic (Timmendorfer Strand / Travemunde): EUR 900-1,800/week. More hotel/apartment oriented than standalone houses.

  • Flensburg: EUR 600-1,000/week. City apartments rather than beach houses.

What to Expect

Danish summer houses are typically spacious, clean, well-maintained, and designed for exactly this kind of family vacation. Many include outdoor terraces, grills, and bike storage. The standard is high. This is one of the things Denmark does better than almost anywhere in Europe for family travel.

Mid-June is the early edge of summer season. You will have good availability and slightly lower prices than July. Book early regardless – the best properties go fast.

8. Weather and Crowds in Mid-June

Weather

  • Temperature: 14-20C (57-68F). Comfortable for exploring. Layers are essential. A warm day is 22C; a cool day is 14C with wind.

  • Daylight: Extraordinary. Sunset after 10 PM. Twilight lingers until nearly midnight. This far north, mid-June days are almost endless. This is a genuine selling point for family travel – long evenings, no rushing to beat darkness.

  • Rain: Possible any day. Pack rain jackets. Showers tend to be brief. This is northern Europe – you accept the weather and go out anyway.

  • Wind: The west coast of Jutland (Romo, Ribe area) can be windy. The Baltic coast (Flensburg east) is more sheltered.

Crowds

Mid-June is the sweet spot for this region:

  • Danish schools are still in session until late June. The big summer rush starts late June / early July.

  • German school holidays vary by state but Schleswig-Holstein typically starts late June.

  • You will have significantly fewer crowds than July-August.

  • Beaches will not be empty but will not be packed.

  • Restaurants and attractions will be open (summer season has started) but not overwhelmed.

This timing is genuinely advantageous. You get summer weather and services without peak-season crowds or prices.

9. The Big Question: Does This Offer Something Genuinely Different?

What It Delivers

Yes, it is genuinely different from Normandy or Zeeland:

  1. Viking history that is alive, not behind glass. The Ribe VikingeCenter is not a museum – it is a living village where kids shoot bows, handle shields, and watch falcons. Nothing in Normandy or Zeeland offers this.

  2. Danish culture is its own thing. The hygge is real. The bakeries, the design sensibility, the laid-back-but-intentional quality of Danish life. Ribe feels different from any French or Dutch town. Flensburg’s Danish-German border identity is genuinely unique.

  3. The endless light. Mid-June at this latitude gives you daylight until midnight. This changes the entire rhythm of family days. Evening beach walks at 9 PM in full sunlight.

  4. Romo’s beach. 4 km wide at low tide. You can drive on it. There is nothing like this on the Dutch, French, or German North Sea coast. It is genuinely dramatic.

  5. The Wadden Sea ecosystem. UNESCO heritage. Mudflat walks, shrimp fishing, tidal nature – similar to what you could see from the Dutch Wadden, but the Danish side is less developed and more wild.

  6. A different pace. Southern Denmark is quieter, slower, less “on the tourist circuit” than Normandy. It rewards the kind of family that likes discovering things rather than checking boxes.

What It Does NOT Deliver

  1. Culinary depth. Normandy wins on food, no contest. Denmark has good bakeries and decent seafood but not the richness of French cuisine.

  2. Ease of access. This is the furthest option. The driving eats into your trip. Normandy is a stretch; this is more so.

  3. Dramatic landscape. No cliffs, no Mont Saint-Michel, no rugged coastline. The landscape is flat – beautiful in a vast, open, sky-meets-sea way, but not dramatic. If you want “wow” landscapes, this is not it.

  4. Mediterranean warmth. The water is cold. The air can be chilly. You are in Scandinavia, and it will occasionally feel like it.

  5. Density of attractions. Normandy has D-Day beaches, Bayeux, Mont Saint-Michel, Honfleur, cider country – all stacked densely. South Jutland has Ribe and Romo and then… you are creating your own days. That is fine for your family (good explorers), but there is less “structure” to fall back on.

Is It “North Sea With More Driving”?

No. It is genuinely different. The Viking dimension, the Danish cultural feel, the summer house tradition, the endless daylight, and Romo’s extraordinary beach – these are not things you get elsewhere in the driving radius. The risk is not that it is the same; the risk is that the driving cost is too high for the reward.

10. Overall Vibe Assessment

Is This Worth the Extra Driving?

Compared to Zeeland (1.5-2 hours from Delft): Zeeland is right there. Zero driving days lost. Good beaches, family-friendly, Dutch. But it is not an adventure. It does not transport you somewhere different. If you want convenience, Zeeland wins. If you want discovery, Denmark wins.

Compared to Normandy (~5-6 hours from Delft): This is the real comparison. Normandy has better food, more dramatic landscapes, denser attractions, and is slightly closer. Denmark has Viking history, a distinctive cultural identity, better beaches for small kids (calmer water), extraordinary daylight, and Legoland as a bonus. Normandy is the safer bet. Denmark is the more adventurous choice.

The Unique Selling Point

The combination of: Ribe’s medieval Viking town + Romo’s epic beach + Danish summer house culture + 10 PM sunsets + Legoland proximity + a cultural world that is neither French nor Dutch nor German but distinctly Scandinavian.

For a family that has already done Alsace, Black Forest, and Bruges – this adds something genuinely new to the collection.

Could a Family Settle Into a Rhythm?

Yes, IF:

  • You have 8+ days total (not 7)

  • You base in or near Ribe

  • You accept that some days are “drive to the beach” and some days are “wander the town”

  • You cook some meals in your summer house with market ingredients

  • You let the rhythm be slow: Viking Centre one day, Romo beach another, Legoland if you want it, a day just exploring Ribe, maybe a day trip to Flensburg

The rhythm would look like: morning bakery run, slow start, one activity or destination, long afternoon, dinner at the house or in town, evening walk in endless daylight.

That is a good week. But it requires giving yourself enough days to get there and back without feeling rushed.

Final Verdict

This is a legitimate wild card that delivers on its promise – but only if you give it enough time. For a 7-day trip, the driving math is painful. For an 8-9 day trip, it becomes genuinely compelling. The combination of Viking history, Danish character, extraordinary beaches, and endless summer light creates something you cannot get closer to Delft.

The biggest risk: arriving after a long drive, spending three days, and feeling like you just got settled when it is time to leave.

Recommendation: If you can stretch to 8-9 days, seriously consider this. Base in Ribe, split weeks between town and Romo beach, overnight in Bremen both ways. If it is a strict 7-day trip, Normandy is probably the better use of your time.
