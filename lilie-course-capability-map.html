Lilie Course Capability Map

A draft mapping of what students can do coming in, what they can do after, and what they believe after each Lilie course. This is a conversation starter for Kyle, Nick, and the academic team — not a finished product. Items marked with (?) are best guesses that need verification.

Entry-Level Courses

Intro to Design and Innovation (BUSI 220)

Faculty: Hesam Panahi — 3 credits

What they can do coming in What they can do after What they believe after
Solve well-defined problems with known answers Empathize with and learn from unfamiliar people and contexts Don’t be afraid to throw away what you built
Write papers and take exams Synthesize messy qualitative data into insights and opportunities Lower the barrier to experimentation and trying new things

(?) Little to no experience interviewing strangers or doing fieldwork Communicate stories, ideas, and reflections to diverse audiences (?) I can make things even if I’m not “a designer”

Core Venture Development Courses

New Enterprises (BUSI 369)

What they can do coming in What they can do after What they believe after

What they can do coming in What they can do after What they believe after

Select and implement a metric aligned to a value creation hypothesis (?) I can build and ship real things, not just assignments
Demo working prototypes and articulate what was learned (?) Speed of learning matters more than speed of building

Specialized / Functional Courses

Tech Product Design and Development / TPDD (BUSI 222)

Faculty: Adam Wulf, Diana Massaro — 3 credits

What they can do coming in What they can do after What they believe after
Interested in tech products but don’t understand the PM role Apply Jobs-To-Be-Done framework to understand customer needs Building, launching, and managing technology products requires the intersectional expertise of technology, business, and design
May have technical skills but haven’t connected them to product strategy Conduct customer interviews and create empathy maps, user personas, and journey maps (?) Good products come from understanding people, not just technology

Build a prototype and run user tests

Business Modeling for Entrepreneurs (BUSI 223)

Faculty: Patrick Ray — 3 credits

What they can do coming in What they can do after What they believe after
May have a business idea but no financial model Translate a startup business plan into a bottoms-up quantitative model of cash flows Creating a financial model for a startup is not the same as the financial model for an existing business
Think of financial modeling as a finance/accounting skill Identify and evaluate key assumptions and risks in a business model You don’t need to have a finance or business background to build a financial model
Haven’t modeled assumptions or tracked them against reality Formulate iterative models with increasing resolution — starting rough, refining over time A financial model is a prototype — developing a model requires being generative, creative, and iterative
(?) Uncomfortable with ambiguity in quantitative work Use the model to track performance, identify errors in assumptions, and adjust based on real data (?) Being messy is okay — “all models are wrong, but some are useful”
Identify appropriate inputs and outputs for a given type of business model (?) A financial model is a decision-making tool, not a prediction machine
Present findings effectively to stakeholders (investors, cofounders, advisors)
Interpret data and constraints in ambiguous circumstances

Financing the Startup Venture (BUSI 461)

Faculty: Zach Ellis, Sophie Randolph — 3 credits

What they can do coming in What they can do after What they believe after
Know startups need funding but don’t understand the options Assess which funding sources best fit a business model — bootstrapping, grants, crowdfunding, angels, accelerators, or VC There are many different ways to finance many different types of businesses
Haven’t interacted with the investment side of startups Build a data-driven TAM/SAM/SOM model that aligns with investor expectations (?) Fundraising is as much art as science — too little runway and you stall, too much dilution and you lose control
Haven’t seen a term sheet or understood deal structure Craft an executive summary and pitch deck that tells a compelling founder story (?) The right financing strategy depends on the type of business you’re building
Read, evaluate, and negotiate term-sheet economics and control provisions (?) Understanding the investor’s perspective makes you a better founder
Spot the dark side of VC — misaligned incentives, dilution traps, and founder pitfalls
Develop a holistic fundraising strategy that balances capital needs, timing, and ownership goals

Entrepreneurial Strategy (BUSI 463)

Faculty: Remington Tonar — 3 credits

What they can do coming in What they can do after What they believe after
Have startup experience or coursework but haven’t studied strategic tradeoffs Choose and implement an entrepreneurial strategy (value chain, asset-based, disruptive, or architectural) for competitive advantage There are many paths through the maze of entrepreneurship and making choices have trade-offs
Haven’t thought systematically about team, equity, and investor decisions Make key strategic choices: who is the customer, what capabilities to use, what kind of company to build, and with whom and how to compete (?) To achieve competitive advantage, entrepreneurs must balance experimentation with strategic commitment
Can identify a startup opportunity but can’t analyze it through a strategy lens Navigate founder dilemmas: whether to go solo or recruit cofounders, how to divide equity, how to manage investor relations, and when to step aside or sell (?) The strategic choices you make early constrain — or enable — everything that comes after
Analyze real startup case studies (Gimlet, Lytro, Netflix, etc.) and develop integrated strategy recommendations (?) Strategy is about choosing what NOT to do, not just what to do
Navigate ethical dilemmas unique to entrepreneurs (fundraising, product safety, growth vs. integrity)
Create a personal pitch deck articulating their own entrepreneurial value

How to Read This Map

“What they can do coming in” = prerequisite capabilities, what students typically arrive with. This helps understand who the course is designed for.

“What they can do after” = demonstrable skills and actions. These are things a student could show someone, not just talk about.

“What they believe after” = mindset shifts, identity changes, internalized beliefs. These are drawn from the Lilie Beliefs document where possible, supplemented with reasonable inferences from syllabi.

Items marked (?) are best guesses to be corrected and filled in.

Generated from full syllabi (Spring 2026 / Fall 2025), the Lilie Beliefs document, and the Lilie Academic Roadmap.
