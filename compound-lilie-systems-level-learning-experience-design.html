Compound Lilie: Systems-Level Learning Experience Design

Extending Compound Learning Experience Design from course-level to institutional-level thinking.

Part 1: The Premise

Kieran Klaassen’s insight in Compound Engineering is deceptively simple: “A system that produces code is more valuable than any individual piece of code.” Replace one word and the premise for Lilie becomes clear:

A system that produces learning experiences is more valuable than any individual course.

The difference matters. A course that improves semester over semester is good. A system that compounds — where every design decision, every faculty observation, every student reflection makes the entire ecosystem permanently smarter — is something else entirely.

The Compound Learning Experience Design note already maps this at the course level: compound ledgers that capture design decisions with the fix and the reason, grading agents that accumulate calibration data, capability handoffs between courses. That work is real and it’s already happening.

But all of it operates within individual courses or the connections between them. None of it addresses the question one level up: does Lilie itself compound?

Part 2: Where Lilie Already Compounds (and Where It Doesn’t)

What Already Exists

Lilie has institutional taste. It’s real. You can feel it in how courses are designed, how faculty talk to students, how the program positions itself relative to traditional academic departments. The taste lives in several places:

The Teaching Manifesto — Eight principles that define Lilie’s identity: 1. The experience matters (ungoogleable wisdom, not commoditized lectures) 2. We recognize the whole student 3. Process matters most in evaluation 4. We experiment to get better 5. Rigorous AND inspiring 6. Collapse the hierarchy — we are approachable 7. Courses are portals to the larger Lilie community 8. Students are navigating ambiguity with courage, and we support that

The First Principles for Venture Creation at Lilie — Pedagogical commitments for the venture creation courses: AI as thinking partner with humans in the loop, accept teams at any stage, minimize the gap between real-world velocity and classroom pace, go beyond traditional customer discovery, support urgency, be an energy amplifier.

Course Design Philosophies — The DBS Course Design Philosophy encodes nine principles drawing on Farza, Jason Fried, Simon Sarris, Unreasonable Hospitality, and TLS. The Intro to Design and Disco courses have their own evolving design documents.

Language Conventions — “Builder” not “student.” “Ship” not “submit.” “Faculty” not “instructor.” These aren’t just preferences; they signal identity and values.

Kyle’s Practices — Priority and care for founders, removal of friction, proactive accountability, not infantilizing entrepreneurship. These live in how Kyle operates, not in a document.

The Gap

All of this exists. None of it is connected. There is no capture → encode → verify loop at the institutional level.

The Teaching Manifesto is aspirational but static. Principle IV literally says “we experiment to get better” — but there is no system for capturing what those experiments taught. When a faculty member discovers that a particular activity transforms the room, where does that knowledge go? Into their memory. When they leave or the semester ends, the lesson goes with them.

The First Principles document captures beliefs, not design patterns. It says what we value but not what we do differently because of those values, or what we tried that didn’t work. The Course User Guides work is starting to address this — but it’s early, and it’s happening course by course, not system-wide.

The 90/10 Problem

Klaassen’s 50/50 rule says half of engineering time should go to improving the system, not just shipping features. Lilie’s ratio is closer to 90/10, and it’s a bandwidth issue, not a choice. Almost all energy goes to running programs — teaching courses, managing co-curriculars, advising students, administering the program. System improvement happens in gaps that rarely exist.

Faculty alignment sessions happen occasionally. Course user guides are being developed. The capability handoff concept is emerging. But all of this is extra — the first thing to get cut when the semester gets busy. System improvement has no structural home at Lilie.

This is exactly the dynamic Klaassen describes: “Work that isn’t a feature feels like distraction — something done with spare time (which never exists).” For Lilie, read: work that isn’t directly serving students feels like a luxury.

Current Stage: Between 0 and 1

Faculty mostly design alone. Shared principles exist but aren’t operationalized. The Teaching Manifesto is referenced but not enforced through structure. Faculty are just starting to design the connections between Lilie’s offerings.

Part 3: The Mapping — Compound Engineering → Compound Lilie

The Compound Learning Experience Design note mapped the analogy at the course level. Here is the systems-level mapping:

Compound Engineering Compound Lilie (Systems Level)
CLAUDE.md captures taste and decisions Lilie’s institutional taste: Manifesto + First Principles + design patterns + language conventions — currently scattered, not connected
Plan → Work → Review → Compound Design → Teach → Assess → Encode
Specialized agents with different expertise Faculty, TAs, co-curricular programs, mentorship network, alumni, competition coaches
The codebase The curriculum + co-curricular ecosystem
Tests and safety nets Capability handoffs, teaching evaluations, student reflections, room-reading
50/50 rule: half the time on system improvement Structural time for improving the learning machine, not just running programs
Extract taste into the system Make implicit “how we do things at Lilie” explicit, inheritable, and compounding
Agent-native architecture: system can see everything Can Lilie “see” student outcomes across all touchpoints — courses, co-curriculars, mentorship, competitions?
Plans are the new code Course user guides as the primary artifact, not individual session plans or syllabi
Three questions before approving output Three questions before approving a course design: What was the hardest decision? What alternatives did you reject? What are you least confident about?

The most interesting parallel might be the last one. Klaassen asks three questions before accepting AI output — designed to surface the thinking behind the work, not just the work itself. What if Lilie asked the same three questions before every course design got finalized? Not as compliance, but as a compound ritual: the answers become permanent institutional knowledge about how design decisions get made here.

Where the Analogy Breaks

The table above presents the mapping cleanly. It’s not clean.

Software systems don’t have egos. A codebase doesn’t resist being refactored. When Klaassen encodes a pattern into CLAUDE.md, the system doesn’t feel threatened — it just gets better. Faculty are not agents. They have identities, creative investments, years of hard-won intuition. Encoding a faculty member’s best teaching moves into a course user guide isn’t like extracting a coding pattern into a config file. It’s more like asking a chef to write down the recipe for the dish they cook by feel — and then telling them someone else will cook it.

Code is also deterministic in a way that teaching isn’t. A well-encoded software pattern produces the same result every time. A well-encoded teaching pattern — “use the paper clip challenge to teach value creation” — produces wildly different outcomes depending on who’s in the room, what energy they bring, what happened ten minutes before. The system can encode what to do; it cannot encode the judgment about whether this is the right moment to do it.

And software compounds continuously — every commit, every merged PR. Teaching compounds in semester-long cycles with months of lag between action and feedback. You can’t A/B test a course design the way you test a feature flag. By the time you know whether an encoded lesson actually improved outcomes, you’ve already designed the next semester.

These aren’t reasons to abandon the analogy. They’re reasons to hold it honestly: compound engineering gives Lilie a vocabulary and a structure, but the domain demands patience, humility about what’s encodable, and respect for the parts of teaching that resist systematization.

Part 4: The Five Stages for Lilie

Klaassen describes five adoption stages for compound engineering. Mapped to Lilie:

Stage 0: Manual Development → Each Faculty Designs Alone

Every course is its own world. Faculty bring their expertise and experience, but there’s no shared system for how courses are designed. Knowledge lives in heads.

Stage 1: Chat-Based Assistance → Shared Principles Exist and Are Referenced

The Teaching Manifesto and First Principles exist. Faculty can reference them. They influence design the way a helpful article influences coding — useful context, but the work is still entirely manual. You know the principles, but they don’t structure your design process.

This is roughly where Lilie is now — between 0 and 1.

Stage 2: Agentic Tools with Line-by-Line Review → Course User Guides Encode Design Decisions

Course user guides capture design reasoning at the course level. Faculty use compound ledgers to record what worked and what didn’t. But faculty still gate every design decision — the guides inform, they don’t generate.

This is analogous to Klaassen’s “gatekeeper” stage: the system has knowledge, but humans approve or reject everything it suggests. For Lilie, this means: the guides tell you why the DP1 dry run exists, but a new faculty member still needs to design their own version of it.

Stages 3–5: Where the Analogy Outruns the Reality

Klaassen’s later stages describe a world where the system generates code from plans (Stage 3), handles entire features autonomously (Stage 4), and runs parallel workstreams from the same compound base (Stage 5).

For software, this trajectory makes sense. For an educational institution where the irreducible human element isn’t a bottleneck to eliminate but the actual product, it’s not clear these stages even apply — or that reaching them would be desirable. A system that can “generate a learning experience from a plan” might produce something that looks right on paper and feels hollow in the room. The later stages of compound engineering optimize for removing the human from the loop. The later stages of compound Lilie might need to optimize for keeping the human in the loop while making the loop smarter.

Worth imagining, not worth planning for. The real work is 0 → 1 → 2.

What Each Transition Actually Requires

0 → 1 (where Lilie is now): Make existing principles visible and referenced in design work. Ensure the Teaching Manifesto and First Principles are actually consulted when designing courses, not just acknowledged. This sounds trivial. It’s not — it requires faculty to see these documents as theirs, not as institutional overhead.

1 → 2: Build course user guides with compound ledgers for every course. Establish the semester-end encoding ritual. Create capability handoff contracts between courses. This is the critical infrastructure step — and it’s the step that the 90/10 bandwidth problem makes hardest. The infrastructure doesn’t build itself, and it doesn’t maintain itself either (see: The Cost of Compounding, below).

Part 5: The Identity Question

This is the hardest part. And it’s personal.

The Engineering Version

Klaassen writes: “Developers subconsciously see AI-assisted development as an identity attack… the code was never really yours. It belongs to the team, the product, and the users.” His reframe: “You did the thinking. All the AI did was the writing.”

The Lilie Version

Course design is self-expression. The choices about what goes on a slide, when to pause, how to phrase a prompt, when to pivot an activity because you’re reading the room — these feel like craft. Like art. Encoding them into a system feels like losing the thing that makes your teaching yours.

This tension exists on two levels:

The personal version. I’ve spent years developing taste — about how to design an experience, how to read a room, how to use language intentionally, how to create the conditions for transformation. Encoding that taste into a document or a system feels like it could flatten it. The compound ledger says “don’t over-explain extreme users — the understanding comes from doing.” But the way I don’t over-explain, the specific judgment call in the moment, the reading of this room on this day — that’s not encodable. Or is it?

The cultural version. Faculty could see systematization as threatening. If the system “knows” how to design a Lilie course, what does that say about the faculty member’s role? If course user guides encode design reasoning so well that a new faculty member can deliver the experience, does that devalue the expertise of the person who created it?

The Voice That Doesn’t Want This

There is a faculty member — maybe real, maybe composite, but real enough — who hears “compound system” and feels something between skepticism and dread. Their teaching is a live performance. They’ve spent a career learning to read a room, to feel when the energy shifts, to know when to abandon the plan and follow the moment. Their best class sessions weren’t designed — they emerged.

Asking them to write that down in a course user guide feels like asking a jazz musician to notate their improvisation. The notation isn’t the music. The guide isn’t the teaching. And the suggestion that it could be — that someone else could read the guide and reproduce the experience — isn’t liberating. It’s insulting. It says: what you do isn’t as special as you think it is. It’s reducible. It’s transferable. It’s encodable.

This person isn’t wrong. A lot of great teaching is irreducible. The question is whether all of it is — or whether some of what feels like irreducible craft is actually repeatable pattern dressed up as intuition. The paper clip challenge works every time not because of the faculty member’s in-the-moment genius, but because the activity structure is good. That structure is encodable. The judgment about when to deploy it and how to adapt it to this room might not be.

The document needs to sit with this tension rather than resolve it. The reframe below is available, but it won’t land for everyone — and forcing it will create resistance that makes compounding harder, not easier.

The Reframe (For Those Ready to Hear It)

Klaassen’s answer: “You did the design thinking. The system just executed the experience.”

The compound system doesn’t replace the faculty member’s judgment. It replaces the re-invention that happens when judgment isn’t captured. Every semester, every new faculty member, every new course currently starts from a version of zero — informed by memory and principles, but not by the accumulated design intelligence of everyone who came before.

The liberating possibility: compound systems free you to do higher-order design work. If the system handles the patterns — the known-good activity structures, the proven language choices, the tested assessment approaches — faculty attention goes to the things that actually require human presence: reading this room, adapting to this moment, seeing this student.

The system handles what should be encoded. The human handles what requires judgment.

But here’s the honest caveat: this reframe works when the faculty member trusts the system and had a hand in building it. Imposed from above, a compound system feels like surveillance. Built collaboratively — “we’re encoding our collective intelligence, not replacing your individual expertise” — it might feel like something worth contributing to. The difference between those two experiences is the difference between a system that compounds and a system that gets ignored.

The open question: Where is the line between encodable design decisions and irreducible human judgment? And does that line move as the system gets smarter? And who gets to decide where the line is — the system designer, or the person whose judgment is being encoded?

Part 6: The Main Loop for Lilie

Klaassen’s loop: Plan → Work → Review → Compound

Lilie’s loop: Design → Teach → Assess → Encode → Verify

Design

Strategic planning informed by encoded institutional knowledge. Not starting from scratch, but from what the system already knows: what worked, what didn’t, what the compound ledger says about this type of activity or this point in the semester.

At the institutional level: which courses run, how they connect, what the capability pipeline looks like, where the co-curricular programs amplify the curricular ones.

Teach

Running the semester. Courses, co-curriculars, mentorship, competitions, speaker series. This is where 90% of energy currently goes — and should. The teaching is the product.

But during teaching, capture happens. Session notes, thinking notes, tic tocs, room-reading observations, TA sync conversations. This raw material already exists. The compound question is: does it go anywhere permanent?

Assess

Multiple feedback channels, not just end-of-semester evaluations: - Student reflections (DP1/DP2, Disco reflections) - Teaching evaluations - TA observations and sync notes - Room-reading in real time - Outcomes data (what did students actually become capable of?) - Gallery crits and demo day performance - Peer feedback

Student reflections are underused as design intelligence. When 8 out of 10 students say “I didn’t understand extreme users until I actually observed one,” that’s not just assessment data — it’s a permanent design lesson.

Encode

The critical step. The semester-end ritual where lessons become permanent design decisions.

What this looks like at the institutional level: - Course user guides absorb specific design decisions — prescriptive, not descriptive. Not “students struggled with X” but “do Y because students struggled with X in Spring 2026.” - Capability handoff contracts update based on what actually transferred between courses - The compound ledger records what the system learned and when - The Teaching Manifesto stays constant (identity); the design patterns evolve (practice)

This is the step that currently doesn’t happen systematically. There’s no protected time for it, no ritual, no shared expectation that encoding is part of the work.

Verify

The missing piece in the current system. The systematic check: - Did the encoded lessons actually change the design? - Did the changed design actually change outcomes?

Without verification, encoding is just documentation. With it, the system has a feedback loop that proves it’s compounding.

Phase Compound Engineering Current Lilie Practice Compound Lilie (Target)
Capture CLAUDE.md updated during work Session notes, thinking notes, tic tocs, TA syncs Same — already strong
Encode Tests, rules, agent instructions Rubrics, course design philosophy, First Principles Compound ledger per course, capability handoff contracts, institutional design patterns
Verify Evals run automatically ??? Semester-end check: did encoded lessons change the design? Did changed design change outcomes?

The Cost of Compounding

Klaassen’s article is an argument for compounding. It doesn’t spend much time on the cost — because in software, the cost is relatively low. Update CLAUDE.md, add a test, merge the PR. Done.

In an educational institution, the cost is higher and ongoing.

Maintenance is the hidden tax. Every compound artifact — every course user guide, every capability handoff contract, every entry in the compound ledger — requires upkeep. A guide that described Spring 2026’s DP1 process accurately becomes misleading by Fall 2026 if the process evolved and nobody updated the guide. A capability handoff contract that says “Intro to Design produces observation skills” but doesn’t reflect a recent curriculum change creates false confidence downstream. A compound system that falls out of sync with reality is worse than having no system at all, because people trust it.

The 90/10 problem isn’t just about finding time to build the compound system. It’s about the permanent commitment to maintain it. Building a course user guide is a one-semester project. Keeping it alive is a multi-year obligation. If Lilie can’t protect time for that ongoing work, the compound system becomes another artifact that was exciting to create and slowly becomes irrelevant — the institutional equivalent of documentation that nobody reads because nobody updates.

Premature encoding creates rigidity. Not every lesson from a semester deserves to be permanent. Some things worked because of the specific students in the room, the specific moment in the culture, the specific energy of a particular week. Encoding those lessons as universal design decisions — “always do X” — creates rules where there should be judgment calls. The compound system needs a way to distinguish between lessons that are genuinely permanent (“the understanding of extreme users comes from doing, not explaining”) and lessons that were contextual (“this cohort responded well to Y”). Not everything that worked should be encoded. Some things should just be remembered.

The system needs to know what it doesn’t know. A compound ledger that only records successes and fixes — “we changed X and it worked” — creates a false picture of certainty. It also needs to record open questions, unresolved tensions, things that worked but nobody is sure why. The most honest compound entry might be: “We tried Z in Spring 2026. It seemed to work. We don’t know if it was the activity or the cohort. Try it again in Fall 2026 and see.”

Part 7: What Lilie’s “CLAUDE.md” Would Look Like

Not one document. A connected system where each part plays a distinct role:

What Stays Constant — Identity and Values

The Teaching Manifesto. The eight principles that define what Lilie is. These shouldn’t change often. They’re the equivalent of a company’s mission statement — the thing you reference to check whether a design decision is ours.

The First Principles for Venture Creation at Lilie. Pedagogical commitments specific to the venture creation courses. The line between “how the game is played outside” and “what we do here” should stay close.

What Evolves — Design Decisions

Course User Guides with Compound Ledgers. One per course. Each guide captures the TLS lever decisions (Space, Language, Activities, Assessment, Roles, Rituals) with the reasoning. The compound ledger records what the course learned each semester — prescriptive entries that accumulate into institutional design intelligence.

What Connects — The Pipeline

Capability Handoff Contracts. Explicit agreements about what each course produces and what the next one assumes. The equivalent of API contracts between services.

Lilie runs many courses — not a single pipeline. But some students take multiple courses over time, and when they do, each course’s output becomes the next one’s input. The handoff contracts make those connections visible rather than accidental. Here’s what one set of connections might look like:

Course Capability In Capability Out Compound Handoff
Intro to Design Curiosity, willingness Observation, synthesis, navigating ambiguity, visual thinking Can observe, frame problems, prototype, work in teams
Disco Observation + synthesis skills Self-awareness, ideation from personal values, venture instinct Knows who they are as a builder, what problems they care about
DBS Self-awareness + venture instinct Shipping capability, evidence-based iteration, builder identity Can go from 0 → 1, ship to real users, make evidence-based decisions

This is one example, not the whole picture. The compound version would map handoff contracts across all Lilie courses and co-curricular programs — identifying where connections exist, where they’re assumed but unverified, and where they’re missing entirely. The table updates each semester based on what actually transferred and what didn’t.

What Signals — Shared Language

Language Guide. Not just a list of preferred words, but the reasons behind each choice. “Builder” not “student” because identity shapes behavior. “Ship” not “submit” because shipping implies an audience and stakes. “Faculty” not “instructor” because instruction is only one part of what we do.

Language choices are the most visible expression of institutional taste. They’re also the easiest to compound — every new language decision and its reasoning gets added to the guide.

What the System Learned — The Institutional Compound Ledger

The meta-document. Every semester, what did the system learn? Not what individual courses learned (that lives in course user guides), but what Lilie as a system discovered:

  • Which capability handoffs worked and which didn’t
  • Where the co-curricular ecosystem amplified the curriculum and where it was disconnected
  • What patterns emerged across multiple courses
  • What the portal network looks like and where it has gaps

Part 8: Provocations and Open Questions

The 50/50 Rule

What if Lilie structurally protected time for system improvement? Not “use your spare time to update course guides” but actual, scheduled, compensated time allocated to compound work — capturing lessons, updating guides, designing the connections between programs, verifying that encoded changes made a difference.

What would Lilie’s version of the 50/50 rule look like? Maybe not 50/50 — maybe 80/20 or 70/30 — but something other than 90/10 with the 10 getting cut when the semester gets busy.

Who Designs the Portal Network?

Principle VII says courses are “portals to the larger Lilie community.” That’s compound thinking hiding in plain sight — it already frames individual courses as nodes in a network. But who designs the network? Who maps which portal connects to which? Who notices when a portal is missing or when two portals could compound each other?

Faculty are just starting to design these connections. The question is whether that work becomes structural or stays occasional.

Does the System Need Hesam in Every Loop?

If the system compounds enough, does it need me in the loop on every design decision? The question is: “Is the scaffolding pattern itself captured as a reusable design primitive I can deploy in Disco or DBS?”

Some decisions require human judgment — reading the room, adapting in the moment, seeing a specific student. Others could be encoded: the order of activities, the language in a prompt, the assessment structure for a particular type of assignment.

The compound question isn’t “can the system replace me?” It’s “what does the system need me for, and what should it handle so I can focus on what only I can do?”

Student Reflections as Institutional Input

Student reflections are underused as design intelligence. Currently they flow one direction: student → grade. The compound version: reflections are systematically mined for design lessons at the institutional level.

When patterns emerge across multiple courses — students consistently say the same thing about navigating ambiguity, or about when they first felt like a builder — that’s not just assessment data. That’s compound input for the institutional system.

The Three Questions

Klaassen asks three questions before accepting AI output: 1. What was the hardest decision you made here? 2. What alternatives did you reject, and why? 3. What are you least confident about?

What if these became standard questions in Lilie’s course design process? Before a course design gets finalized for the semester, the designing faculty answers these three questions. The answers become permanent institutional knowledge about how design decisions get made — not just what was decided, but why, and what the alternatives were.

Over time, this accumulates into a rich archive of design reasoning that any faculty member can reference. Not “here’s what to do” but “here’s how people at Lilie think about design decisions, and here’s what they’ve rejected and why.”

The Provocation

Every semester Lilie runs that doesn’t make the next semester’s design permanently better at the institutional level is a missed compounding opportunity. Individual courses may improve. Individual faculty may grow. But the system — the machine that produces learning experiences — stays roughly the same.

The question isn’t “are we getting better?” It’s: “What does the system now know that it didn’t know before — and can anyone at Lilie access that knowledge?”
